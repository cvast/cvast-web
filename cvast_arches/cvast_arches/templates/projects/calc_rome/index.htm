Capturing the Lifecycle of Ceramics in Rome

Rome, Italy

Rachel Opitz, USF Assistant Professor in Anthropology and at CVAST is collaborating with archaeologists Marcello Mogetta at the University of Missouri and Laura Banducci at Carleton University to investigate traces of past use seen on Black Gloss ceramics from the Sala V collection of Rome’s Capitoline Museum.

Black gloss pottery was made in central and southern Italy from the late 4th century BCE through to the middle of the 1st century BCE and plays an important role in our understanding of the consumption of food and drink both every day and on special occasions. The objects in the Sala V collection, normally in storage and not accessible by the public, have never been studied.

Damien Vurpillot and Valerie Taillandier, doctoral students at UBFC, using the GOM blue light scanner to scan a vessel from the Sala V collection

Why study use-wear on Roman ceramics? What are the broader questions?

Where did these vessels come from, and how were they chosen to be placed into the tomb of a friend or family member, or left in a sanctuary as an offering? Were they family heirlooms, bought purely for the purpose of burial or votive deposit, or used in a ritual before being placed? How can better information about use of these vessels contribute to our understanding of the purchasing, use and deposition of artefacts in the Roman world, and the Roman economy in general? Is there a correlation between the quality of the table wares and the level of use wear? Were imported objects used differently than the locally made ones?

Marks from cutting the vessel off of the wheel

These broad cuts are quite distinct from those we see on its interior due to abrasions from knives during food consumption.

How can you measure and characterize wear on ceramics?

To address these questions, our team has been carrying out 3D scanning to capture detailed surface models of the objects. These models are now being analysed to assess the types and extent of wear and marks of use on their interior and exterior surfaces. These marks might indicate things that happened during the production process, during the active use of the object, or after its deposit in a grave or sanctuary. Differentiating between these types of wear is crucial, as is identifying further specific traces of different types of wear that can be linked to actions like cutting up food or stirring liquids.

Cut marks on the interior of a plate

This wear is most likely caused by the repeated consumption of food.

What has been learned?

In the first stage of the project we are developing metrics to differentiate between areas dominated by specific types of wear, e.g. scratches made by a sharp narrow tool vs. broad shallow abrasions, with promising initial results. Current research is focused on developing new ways to measure and quantify these different types of wear, so that analyses can be performed across large datasets and more consistent assessment of the extent and type of use- wear can be made.
