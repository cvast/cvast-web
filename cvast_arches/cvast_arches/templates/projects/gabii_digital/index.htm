The Gabii Project - Digital Gabii

Gabii, Italy

Rachel Opitz, USF Assistant Professor in Anthropology and at CVAST, is one of the directors of The Gabii Project, an international archaeological initiative launched in 2007 with the objective of studying and excavating the ancient Latin city of Gabii, a city-state that was both a neighbor of, and a rival to, Rome in the first millennium BC. The Gabii Project’s core objective is a fresh look at the phenomenon of urbanism. Within the churn of societies experimenting with urban life, the Roman case stands out for its longevity and broad impact, widespread in its heyday and exerting critical influence a thousand years later during the emergence modern Western urbanism.

All digital data collection is carried out by the topography team and students in the Gabii Project’s field school

Central research questions on Gabii and urbanism

How did this particularly successful model for urbanism develop? What new insights into Roman and Italic society can be gleaned from a fresh look at critical moments in which towns and cities in central Italy are just getting started, in which it is not yet clear how to build a city or live an urban life? The Gabii Project is dedicated to gathering the evidence needed to address these longstanding questions.

An infant burial, documented using Structure From Motion, part of the Gabii Project’s standard recording method

Creating Gabii's digital record

Dr. Opitz leads the project’s topography team, who are responsible for the projects all-digital recording strategy. Since 2009, the project has developed and implemented a recording method that integrates structure from motion (SFM) to create georeferenced 3D models and a tablet-based recording system.

The user interface for the Gabii Project’s forthcoming Digital Publication

Digital data, interpretation and communication

The digital recording strategy is critical to the success of the project. Rapid 3D recording allows the team to excavate at a large scale, investigate multiple facets of the urban fabric at Gabi, and create a detailed and accessible record. Dr. Opitz also leads the Project’s Digital Publication initiative, which is integrating publication of the project’s complete digital archive and 3D models into the ​Gabii Project Reports, a series of digital volumes on the results and findings of the Gabii Project excavations.
