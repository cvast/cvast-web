Herbert Maschner (Executive Director)

Herbert Maschner is Executive Director of Center for Virtualization and Applied Spatial Technologies (CVAST) at the University of South Florida. He also holds tenured Professorships in the USF Department of Anthropology and the School of Geosciences. He serves as the Academic Editor of PeerJ, and on the Editorial Boards of the Journals Digital Applications in Archaeology and Cultural Heritage and Science and Technology of Archaeological Research (STAR); He is an elected Fellow of the American Association for the Advancement of Science and serves as the Society for American Archaeology representative to the AAAS.

The recipient of over 13 million in grants, including PI on ≈$5.9 million from the National Science Foundation, he has published 2 monographs, 9 edited books, 108 articles, chapters, and reviews, and presented over 200 conference papers with abstracts. Research areas include virtualization, visualization, informatics, 3D scanning, and public science; the creation of digital research infrastructures that transcend humanities, physical, natural, and social sciences; digital heritage, digital humanities, museum and research informatics, and digital natural history at regional and global scales. Other current specialties in human biocomplexity and the environment, resource and community sustainability, long- term human impacts and interactions with marine ecosystems, fisheries, ocean modeling, and human ecosystem engineering. Theoretical specialties in evolutionary psychology, warfare and inequality, global historical ecologies, public education and the democratization of science, and virtual museums and repositories. Methodological interests in 3d imaging, digital heritage, and database construction, elemental and isotopic analyses, geographic information systems, social networks, and complex systems analysis. Primary field research areas are the Arctic, Western North America, and the Mediterranean.

Dr. Davide Tanasi (Assistant Professor, History)

Dr. Davide Tanasi is an archaeologist specialized in Mediterranean prehistory and archaeology of Greek and Roman Sicily and Malta. After an initial academic training in Classical Studies, in 2007 he received his PhD in Classical Archaeology from the University of Torino, Italy. In the past decade he has embraced the study of computer graphics applied to archaeology with special interest in 3D Digital Imaging and digital communication of cultural heritage. In 2006 he co-founded the research program of computer graphics applied to archaeology Archeomatica Project at the University of Catania’s Image Processing Laboratory of the Department of Mathematics and Computer Science and since then he serves as co-Scientific Director. Between 2012 and 2015 he directed the Laboratory of Virtual Archaeology at the Arcadia University Sicily Center of Siracusa (Italy). He is currently Affiliate Assistant Professor at the Department of Classics of University of Washington, Fellow of the College of Global Studies of Arcadia University and founding partner and Member of the Board of Trustees of the Sicily Center for International Education of Siracusa, Sicily (SCIE), an Italian third-party study abroad provider for American universities. Since 2014 he is Section Editor for Archaeological Science for the international scientific journal Open Archaeology (Walter De Gruyter publisher) and he is also founding co-editor of the book series The Heritage of Western Greece (Parnassos Press). He has consistently published in high-impact peer-reviewed journals and worked with international publishers as editor/author for miscellaneous monographs. He also co-produced, as scriptwriter, scientific consultant and assistant director, documentaries in 3D computer animation, among which the multi-awarded movies about Greek Siracusa (Siracusa 3D Reborn) and Roman Pompeii (Pompei 3D. A Buried Story).

Dr. Jen Bright (Assistant Professor, Geosciences)

Dr. Jen Bright (Assistant Professor, Geosciences) has a PhD in functional morphology from the University of Bristol, and a MGeol from the University of Leeds. She was most recently an ERC Postdoctoral Research Assistant University of Sheffield; Project Title “Understanding evolutionary rates on the Tree of Life in time and space." She is the author or co-author on 10 major publications in journals such as the Journal of Anatomy, Biology Letters, and the Journal of Paleontology. What made Jen a premier candidate for this position is her work in crowd-sourcing digital analyses and her pioneering work in Finite Element Analysis for paleontology and evolutionary anatomy. Using laser scans of bird beaks and other data, she has participated in the transformation of digital paleontology.

As part of a team, she has led the morphometrics component of the research to collect and landmark white- and blue-light surface scans (FlexScan3D) of the beak of every bird species, obtained from museum collections. The immense number of specimens involved led her to develop a crowdsourcing website (www.markmybird.org), allowing the public to participate in data collection and interact with the research. The site has been hugely successful, and in the months since its launch, they have registered over 600 users who have marked more than 7,000 specimens between them. The data collected is being used to investigate macroevolutionary patterns by mapping the morphometric data on to a phylogenetic tree.

Using a combination of Finite Element Analysis and Geometric Morphometrics methods, she is conducting research to discern which aspects of form are under biomechanical, phylogenetic, or developmental control, to give greater confidence in our ability to understand the ecological role and behavior of extinct taxa known solely from their skeletal form. This permits detailed questions about the nature of faunal changes through time. Although her primary interests concern bird skulls, the methods she uses are easily transferable to other taxa or non-biological objects and systems.

Dr. Rachel Opitz (Assistant Professor, Anthropology)

Dr. Rachel Opitz (Assistant Professor, Anthropology) began her archaeological career in Europe, completing a PhD at Cambridge and then working as a post- doctoral researcher in France (Besançon). She went to the University of Arkansas through the Digital Institute for Archaeology in 2011 as a post-doctoral fellow and then returned to Europe in 2012 to continue her research, taking up post-doctoral fellowships in Slovenia and France in 2012-2013 in connection with ongoing projects in the forests of eastern France. She then returned to the University of Arkansas in the role of Executive Director of the SPARC Program, CAST’s NSF-funded effort to develop a national center for research in spatial archaeometry. This year, in addition to continuing to manage the SPARC Program, and she was serving as a Visiting Assistant Professor in Anthropology.

Rachel has an active research program focused on rural western Mediterranean societies and landscapes in the 1st millennium BCE, a strong track record in publication with a book contract in hand and a dozen peer reviewed articles, ongoing engagement in the direction of a two research programs - SPARC and the Gabii Project - and experience teaching and mentoring students at the undergraduate and graduate level. Her research investigates how different rural communities are operating within this increasingly interconnected world. The foundations of this work are in remote sensing and survey, human perception of the built and natural environment as studied through formal exercises in 3D modeling and analysis of visual attention of users of the models, and the material culture of rural communities and the small towns emerging within them. The themes of her research cut across regional divides, providing natural links to the research and teaching of colleagues.

She has an active research project in eastern France, carrying out multi-method survey in the Forêt de Chailluz in the Franche-Comté and she is associated with comparative projects in the Forêt de Haye (Lorraine) and Nages (Languedoc). Through this project (Project LIEPPEC) she is applying a multi-method approach to understand the changing dynamics of the area which is now within and immediately around the Forêt de Chailluz, integrating airborne lidar, geophysical prospection, palynology, archival and documentary evidence, metal-detector prospection and limited coring. Perfect for CVAST, she has recognized expertise in photogrammetric modeling in the context of excavations, LIDAR applications to sites and landscapes, and in developing information metrics to assess the appropriateness of 3D data for different types of archaeological interpretation. She is also invested in the application of perceptual saliency metrics for investigating the way humans visually perceive the basic elements of their surroundings, with important implications for the organization of space in built-up areas and the placement of structures and markers in landscapes.

She has developed a strong international research network, notably as the chair of the Aerial Archaeology Research Group (2015-2018), a permanent member of the ArchaeoLandscapes International Project General Management Board and as the Director of SPARC. In addition to participation in theses research networks, and presenting and publishing on methodological topics and collaborative digital research methods in international venues, she is co-PI on a NEH Digital Humanities Start-Up grant on the scholarly peer review and publication of 3D models, in connection with her ongoing work as Head of Digital Data for the Gabii Project.

Dr. Ryan Carney (Assistant Professor, Integrative Biology)

Dr. Ryan Carney (Assistant Professor, Integrative Biology) has two bachelor’s degrees from UC Berkeley, one in Integrative Biology and one in Art. He has a both a MPH and an MBA from Yale in health and technology. He has a MS and PhD from Brown University in Ecology and Evolutionary Biology. As a National Geographic Explorer, and with papers published as a graduate student in Nature, Nature Communications, Paleontology, Scientific Reports (Nature), and multiple papers in Emerging Infectious Diseases, he is already established. He has over 600 citations as a graduate student.

At Yale, Ryan created the most successful and most cited geospatial predictive model for anticipating the spread of dengue favor, a mosquito born virus. This research effort will be key to modeling and managing the spread of the Zika virus in Florida and Dr. Carney’s skills are already in demand for this work.

At Brown University, Ryan primarily studied the evolution of flight and locomotion. He studies the structural and functional changes that occurred during major locomotor transitions, as well as the evolution and function of coloration over deep time. His interdisciplinary research program combined leading-edge digital and molecular technologies, exceptional fossil specimens, and novel comparative approaches among living and extinct animals. His work aims to reveal overarching evolutionary processes that have driven adaptive radiations, and answer fundamental questions in organismal biology. He also brings a diverse background and over a decade of leadership experience serving as Principal Investigator for various large-scale research projects. As a paleontologist and evolutionary anatomist, his research interests include:

  • Discovering new anatomical insights into the evolution of avian flight, with an emphasis on Archaeopteryx
  • Advancing understanding of joint functional morphology and locomotor biomechanics, using in vivo methods
  • Developing a new analytical framework for evolutionary comparisons of anatomy and motion
  • Reconstructing ancient motion, through a synthesis of the above three approaches and animation software
  • Reconstructing original coloration in the fossil record, to reveal evolutionary insights
  • Developing new advanced materials through biomimicry
  • Integrating research into educational tools, open-source software, and outreach
  • Exploring the research/educational potential of next-generation platforms (virtual reality, interactive holograms).

Matthew Vincent (Research Associate)

Matthew Vincent is a Research Associate in CVAST and manager of our European operations. He is based at the Bruno Kessler Foundation in Trento, Italy. He has been digging in the dirt since 2004 when he got his first taste of archaeology in Jordan. It wasn’t long after that Matthew began mixing another passion into archaeology: technology. Beginning with a focus on GIS in archaeology, he then began to work on field recording, photogrammetry, laser scanning, and immersive visualization systems. In 2014 Matthew joined the Initial Training Network for Digital Cultural Heritage, a Marie Sklowdowska-Curie Actions program focused on holistic approaches to cultural heritage using advanced technology. In 2015, Matthew founded Rekrei (formerly Project Mosul) with fellow researcher Chance Coughenour. As part of CVAST, Matthew will focus on the European projects, helping to coordinate those while also working on continued research in crowdsourcing lost heritage through Rekrei, cultural heritage data platforms related to CVAST work in Europe, streamlining data acquisition and processing, and innovative forms of 3D data dissemination. His specializations include: photogrammetry, databases, archaeology, 3D acquisition, drones, web-based applications, mobile applications, crowdsourcing, and public archaeology. He has a BA Theology & Biblical Languages from Walla Walla University, 2004; MA Anthropological Archaeology from University of California, San Diego, 2013 (NSF IGERT Fellow); PhD Candidate at the University of Murcia in the program of History, Geography and Art History: Society, Territory, and Culture - Fall 2016. He is currently leading a number of projects in Jordan.

Dr. Jeffrey P. Du Vernay (Research Assistant Professor)

Dr. Jeff Du Vernay is a Research Assistant Professor in CVAST who specializes in close and middle range 3D scanning, 3D modeling and visualization software applications, 3D printing, and GIS. His primary research interest is the archaeology of the Southeastern United States (particularly Florida), but has contributed to research projects at CVAST and elsewhere at diverse locations including the U.S Midwest and Mid-Atlantic regions, U.S. Virgin Islands, Hawaii, Armenia, France, Mexico, and Guatemala.
